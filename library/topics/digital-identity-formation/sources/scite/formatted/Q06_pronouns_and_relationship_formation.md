## Pronoun Use as a Predictor of Dyadic Relationship Outcomes

Research demonstrates that pronoun use serves as a significant linguistic marker for predicting relationship formation and sustained engagement across various dyadic contexts. The evidence reveals consistent patterns linking specific pronoun usage to relationship quality and therapeutic outcomes.


### Therapeutic Alliance and Clinical Contexts

In therapeutic settings, pronoun patterns strongly predict alliance quality and treatment success,. First-person plural pronoun use by therapists correlates with stronger therapeutic alliances, while patients' increased first-person singular pronoun usage negatively correlates with alliance strength. Language style matching between therapists and patients, including pronoun coordination, effectively predicts therapeutic alliance development. Additionally, pronoun use tracks treatment outcomes, with changes in first-person singular usage predicting symptom improvement in depression and anxiety interventions.
  - Citations: Huang et al. (2025), Ryu et al. (2023), Li et al. (2023)


### Marital and Romantic Relationships

Marital research consistently shows that couples using more "we" pronouns and fewer "you" pronouns report greater satisfaction and lower divorce rates. Spouses employing more first-person plural pronouns during conflict resolution produce more positive problem solutions, while second-person pronoun use correlates with increased negativity. However, the relationship between pronoun use and outcomes depends on situational factors and interactional contexts,.
  - Citations: Ireland et al. (2010), Simmons et al. (2005), Rohrbaugh et al. (2008), Davis (2019)


### Customer Service and Online Communities

In customer service interactions, personal pronoun use significantly impacts customer perceptions and engagement. The strategic use of different personal pronouns signals varying relationship dynamics and affects customer responses. In online mental health communities, pronoun patterns indicate user engagement levels, with reduced second and third-person pronoun use suggesting decreased social interaction.
  - Citations: Packard et al. (2018), Choudhury et al. (2016)
