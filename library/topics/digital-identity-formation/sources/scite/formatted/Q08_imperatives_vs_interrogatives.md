# Imperative versus Interrogative Constructions in Initial Requests: Predicting Interaction Outcomes


## Introduction

The relationship between linguistic construction choice and interaction outcomes represents a fundamental area of inquiry in pragmatics and conversation analysis. While imperative and interrogative constructions serve as primary vehicles for making requests, their differential effects on interaction satisfaction, success rates, and return behavior remain underexplored in the literature. This synthesis examines the available evidence regarding how these distinct grammatical forms influence interactional dynamics and outcomes.


## Theoretical Framework: Construction Types and Speech Acts

The distinction between imperative and interrogative constructions in request-making contexts operates at multiple linguistic levels. As established in the literature, "declarative," "interrogative," and "imperative" function as grammatical labels, while "statement," "command," and "question" describe types of speech acts, with major sentence types corresponding to these categories and being found in every language. This fundamental distinction provides the theoretical foundation for understanding how different grammatical forms may produce varying interactional effects.
  - Citations: Aikhenvald (2015)

Research demonstrates that interrogative constructions can serve multiple functions beyond their canonical question-asking role. Interrogative sentences are not only used for asking questions but also for requests or orders. Similarly, declarative sentences, in addition to being used to convey news, can also function to ask questions, while interrogative sentences can function for requests or orders. This functional flexibility suggests that the choice between imperative and interrogative forms may carry significant pragmatic implications for interaction outcomes.
  - Citations: Muzakki (2018), Firdaus (2019)


## Empirical Evidence on Construction Choice and Interaction Dynamics


### Medical Reception Contexts

The most direct evidence regarding the relationship between construction choice and interaction outcomes comes from healthcare settings. Research on general practice receptions reveals that variation in how receptionists initiated triaging—specifically, how they sought to establish whether requests were urgent enough for same-day appointments—was consequential for the smooth progress of interactions. When receptionists initiated triaging with polar interrogatives asking patients about urgency, this approach demonstrated measurable effects on interaction flow and efficiency.
  - Citations: Sikveland (2016)

This finding suggests that interrogative constructions, when used as initial request responses, may facilitate more effective information gathering and decision-making processes compared to alternative approaches. The structured nature of interrogative responses appears to create clearer pathways for interaction progression, potentially leading to higher satisfaction rates and more successful outcomes.


### Educational Contexts and Directive Speech Acts

Educational settings provide additional insights into the relationship between construction choice and interaction effectiveness. Analysis of teacher-student interactions reveals that educators employ multiple construction types strategically. Research shows that teachers use declarative forms with 74 utterances (50%), imperative forms with 50 utterances (34%), and interrogative forms with 23 utterances (16%). The predominance of declarative forms suggests that indirect approaches may be preferred in educational contexts, potentially due to their perceived politeness and reduced face-threatening nature.
  - Citations: Mustikasari (2016)

Furthermore, the forms of directive speech acts in educational settings include declarative, interrogative, and imperative constructions, with interrogatives being used for purposes including suggestions, questions, requests, commands, prohibitions, and reminders. This pattern indicates that interrogative constructions may offer greater versatility and social acceptability in institutional contexts, potentially leading to better interaction outcomes and sustained engagement.
  - Citations: Setiawan (2025)


### Developmental and Social Adaptation Patterns

Research on children's request development provides crucial insights into the social effectiveness of different construction types. Children start to use conditional verb forms and interrogatives as social adaptations for making requests to equal peers, with requesting developing from embodied, co-constructed action to independent linguistic formulations between ages one and five. This developmental pattern suggests that interrogative constructions represent a more socially sophisticated approach to request-making, potentially associated with better peer relationships and social outcomes.
  - Citations: Laakso (2021)

The finding that children naturally gravitate toward interrogative forms when interacting with peers indicates that these constructions may be inherently more effective for maintaining social harmony and achieving compliance in egalitarian relationships. This has implications for adult interactions where power dynamics are less pronounced or where relationship maintenance is prioritized.


## Construction-Specific Interaction Patterns


### Imperative Constructions and Directness

Research on family conversations reveals that imperative expressions constitute 88% of all directive forms in domestic settings. This high frequency suggests that imperative constructions may be more effective in contexts where directness is valued and social distance is minimal. The prevalence of imperative forms in intimate settings indicates that these constructions may produce satisfactory outcomes when relationship maintenance concerns are reduced.
  - Citations: Nurhidayah (2024)

However, the dominance of imperative forms in family contexts may not translate to other interaction types. The contextual specificity of construction effectiveness suggests that the relationship between grammatical form and interaction outcomes is mediated by social and situational factors.


### Interrogative Constructions and Politeness

The literature consistently demonstrates that interrogative constructions serve important politeness functions. Research shows that interrogative forms are used to represent requesting, clarifying whether students have understood, and giving warnings. This multifunctionality suggests that interrogative constructions may produce better interaction outcomes by allowing speakers to accomplish multiple social goals simultaneously.
  - Citations: - (2015)

The use of interrogative constructions appears particularly effective in contexts requiring face-saving strategies. The indirect nature of interrogative requests may reduce the perceived imposition on recipients, potentially leading to higher compliance rates and more positive interaction evaluations.


## Implications for Interaction Outcomes


### Satisfaction and Compliance

The evidence suggests that interrogative constructions may generally produce higher satisfaction rates due to their perceived politeness and reduced directness. The finding that interrogative forms are preferred in educational and professional contextsindicates that these constructions may be associated with more positive interaction evaluations and sustained engagement.
  - Citations: Setiawan (2025), Mustikasari (2016)


### Return Behavior and Relationship Maintenance

The developmental evidence showing children's progression toward interrogative forms for peer interactionssuggests that these constructions may be more effective for maintaining ongoing relationships. The social sophistication required for interrogative request-making may translate to better long-term interaction outcomes and increased likelihood of return behavior.
  - Citations: Laakso (2021)


### Context-Dependent Effectiveness

The research demonstrates that construction effectiveness varies significantly across contexts. While imperative forms dominate in family settings, interrogative forms are preferred in institutional contexts. This pattern suggests that optimal construction choice depends on relationship type, power dynamics, and social expectations, with implications for predicting interaction success.
  - Citations: Nurhidayah (2024), Setiawan (2025), Mustikasari (2016)


## Conclusion

The available evidence suggests that interrogative constructions generally produce more favorable interaction outcomes compared to imperative constructions, particularly in formal and institutional contexts. The greater politeness, multifunctionality, and social sophistication associated with interrogative forms appear to translate into improved satisfaction rates and more effective interaction management. However, the relationship between construction choice and outcomes is highly context-dependent, with imperative forms showing effectiveness in intimate settings where directness is valued. Future research should focus on systematic comparison of construction types across diverse interaction contexts to establish more definitive causal relationships between grammatical form and interaction outcomes.
